Timeline

Key Events in Nellie Bly's Life and Career

May 5, 1864

Birth

Born Elizabeth Jane Cochran in Cochran's Mills, Pennsylvania (now Burrell Township). She was one of fifteen children in the family (Kroeger, 23).

Historical Context: Born during the Civil War, in an era when women had very limited rights and career opportunities.
1880

Father's Death and Financial Hardship

Michael Cochran dies, leaving the family in financial difficulty. Elizabeth's formal education is cut short, and the family struggles financially (Kroeger, 31).

1885

Career Begins

Writes a fiery response to a Pittsburgh Dispatch article titled "What Girls Are Good For," which argued women should stick to domestic roles (Kroeger, 67). Editor George Madden is impressed and hires her as a reporter.

Significance: This marks the beginning of her journalism career and her lifelong advocacy for women's rights.

Adopts "Nellie Bly" Pen Name

Takes the name "Nellie Bly" from a Stephen Foster song. During this period, she writes about women's rights, labor issues, and social problems.

1886

Mexico Correspondence

Travels to Mexico as a foreign correspondent, writing about government corruption and the lives of common people. Her critical reporting makes her unwelcome, and she's forced to return to the United States.

1887

Moves to New York

Leaves Pittsburgh for New York City, seeking better opportunities. Initially struggles to find work in the competitive New York journalism market.

September 1887

Hired by New York World

Joseph Pulitzer's New York World hires her after she pitches the idea of going undercover in an asylum to expose conditions. This marks the beginning of her most famous work.

September 25, 1887

"Ten Days in a Mad-House" Begins

Feigns mental illness to gain admission to the Women's Lunatic Asylum on Blackwell's Island. Endures ten days of horrific conditions, abuse, and neglect (Bly, "Ten Days in a Mad-House," 1887).

Revolutionary Aspect: First major undercover journalism assignment, establishing a new form of investigative reporting.
October 9, 1887

Asylum Exposé Published

The New York World publishes her asylum exposé, shocking the public and authorities. The articles create immediate outcry for reform (New York World, October 9, 1887).

Immediate Impact: Grand jury investigation ordered, $1 million budget increase for mental health care approved.
1888

Continued Investigative Work

Continues undercover investigations, exposing corruption in employment agencies, dangerous working conditions in factories, and other social problems.

November 14, 1889

Around the World Journey Begins

Departs New York on the steamship Augusta Victoria, attempting to break the fictional record set in Jules Verne's "Around the World in Eighty Days" (Goodman, 45).

Global Attention: The journey captures international media attention and becomes a worldwide sensation.
December 1889 - January 1890

Journey Highlights

  • London: Meets Jules Verne himself
  • France: Visits Amiens and receives Verne's blessing
  • Egypt: Travels through the Suez Canal
  • Asia: Visits Ceylon (Sri Lanka), Singapore, Hong Kong, and Japan
  • Pacific: Crosses the Pacific Ocean to San Francisco
January 25, 1890

Completes World Journey

Arrives back in New York after traveling 24,899 miles in exactly 72 days, 6 hours, 11 minutes and 14 seconds—beating the fictional record and her competitor Elizabeth Bisland (Goodman, 298).

Cultural Impact: Becomes an international celebrity and inspiration for women worldwide.
1890

Book Publication

Publishes "Around the World in Seventy-Two Days," documenting her journey and experiences (Bly, "Around the World in Seventy-Two Days," 1890).

1895

Marriage

Marries Robert Seaman, a millionaire industrialist 40 years her senior. Takes a temporary break from journalism to manage his business interests.

1904

Husband's Death and Business Management

Robert Seaman dies, leaving Bly to manage his manufacturing companies. She implements progressive labor policies including healthcare benefits and libraries for employees.

1914-1918

World War I Correspondence

Serves as a war correspondent in Europe during World War I, reporting on the Eastern Front and refugee conditions.

1919

Return to New York Journalism

Returns to New York and continues writing for various newspapers, focusing on social issues and women's rights until her health begins to decline.

January 27, 1922

Death

Dies of pneumonia in New York City at age 57. Despite her groundbreaking career and lasting impact, her death receives relatively little media attention.

Legacy: Leaves behind a revolutionary approach to journalism and a lasting impact on women's rights and social reform.

Timeline Summary: Revolution, Reaction, and Reform

Revolution (1885-1890)

Nellie Bly revolutionizes journalism through undercover reporting and breaks gender barriers in the field. Her asylum exposé and world journey establish new standards for investigative reporting and women's capabilities.

Reaction (1887-1895)

Public and institutional response to her work ranges from enthusiastic support to harsh criticism. Her methods spark debates about journalism ethics and women's proper roles in society.

Reform (1887-Present)

Her investigations lead to concrete changes in mental health care, journalism standards, and women's professional opportunities. The reforms she sparked continue to influence society today.
