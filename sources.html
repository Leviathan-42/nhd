Sources

Research Bibliography and Documentation

This National History Day project on Nellie Bly draws from a variety of primary and secondary sources to provide a comprehensive view of her life, work, and impact. The sources are organized by type and annotated to explain their relevance to the Revolution, Reaction, and Reform theme.

Primary Sources

These sources were created during Nellie Bly's lifetime or contain her own words and writings.

Bly, Nellie. "Ten Days in a Mad-House." New York World, October 9, 1887.

Relevance: This is Bly's original exposé that sparked major reforms in mental health care. Essential primary source documenting both the revolutionary reporting methods and the conditions that needed reform.

Theme Connection: Revolution (new reporting techniques), Reaction (public outrage), Reform (asylum improvements)

Bly, Nellie. Around the World in Seventy-Two Days. New York: Pictorial Weeklies Company, 1890.

Relevance: Bly's firsthand account of her record-breaking journey, demonstrating her revolutionary approach to challenging gender limitations and societal expectations.

Theme Connection: Revolution (breaking barriers), Reaction (international attention), Reform (changing perceptions of women's capabilities)

Bly, Nellie. "Trying to Be a Servant." New York World, October 30, 1887.

Relevance: Another undercover investigation exposing exploitation of domestic workers, showing Bly's consistent focus on social justice issues.

Theme Connection: Revolution (investigative methods), Reform (labor conditions awareness)

Letters and Correspondence from Nellie Bly Collection, Carnegie Library of Pittsburgh.

Relevance: Personal correspondence revealing Bly's motivations, challenges, and perspectives on her work and its impact.

Theme Connection: Provides insight into the personal side of revolutionary change and reform efforts.

New York World newspaper articles and editorial responses, 1887-1890.

Relevance: Contemporary newspaper coverage showing immediate public and editorial reactions to Bly's work.

Theme Connection: Documents the reaction phase of the theme, showing both support and criticism.

Secondary Sources

Scholarly works and modern analyses that provide historical context and interpretation of Bly's impact.

Kroeger, Brooke. Nellie Bly: Daredevil, Reporter, Feminist. New York: Times Books, 1994.

Relevance: Comprehensive biography providing detailed analysis of Bly's life and career, with extensive use of primary sources and historical context.

Theme Connection: Analyzes all three aspects of the theme throughout Bly's career and legacy.

Goodman, Matthew. Eighty Days: Nellie Bly and Elizabeth Bisland's History-Making Race Around the World. New York: Ballantine Books, 2013.

Relevance: Detailed examination of Bly's around-the-world journey and its cultural impact, including the competitive aspect with Elizabeth Bisland.

Theme Connection: Focus on the revolutionary nature of the journey and its impact on women's roles.

Baker, Jean H. "Votes for Women: The Struggle for Suffrage Revisited." American Historical Review, vol. 120, no. 3, 2015, pp. 98-118.

Relevance: Scholarly analysis placing Bly's work within the broader context of women's rights movements in the late 19th century.

Theme Connection: Contextualizes Bly's revolutionary impact on women's professional opportunities.

Sloan, William David. "The Development of American Journalism." Journalism History, vol. 25, no. 2, 1999, pp. 67-82.

Relevance: Academic examination of how investigative journalism evolved, with specific attention to Bly's pioneering methods.

Theme Connection: Analyzes the revolutionary impact on journalism and subsequent reforms in media standards.

Grabe, Maria Elizabeth. "The Power of Communication: Managing Information in Public Organizations." Public Administration Review, vol. 63, no. 4, 2003, pp. 478-492.

Relevance: Analysis of how media exposure like Bly's asylum reporting creates pressure for institutional reform.

Theme Connection: Directly addresses the reform aspect of the theme through examination of policy changes.

Government Documents and Official Records

Official documents showing the concrete reforms and changes resulting from Bly's work.

New York City Department of Public Charities and Correction Annual Report, 1888.

Relevance: Official government document detailing the reforms implemented at Blackwell's Island following Bly's exposé.

Theme Connection: Concrete evidence of the reform impact of Bly's revolutionary reporting.

Grand Jury Report on Blackwell's Island Asylum, October 1887. New York County Courthouse Records.

Relevance: Official investigation triggered by Bly's reporting, documenting findings and recommendations for reform.

Theme Connection: Shows the direct link between Bly's revolutionary methods and concrete reforms.

New York State Budget Appropriations for Mental Health Institutions, 1887-1890. New York State Archives.

Relevance: Financial records showing the $1 million budget increase for mental health care following Bly's exposé.

Theme Connection: Quantifiable evidence of reform resulting from revolutionary journalism.

Digital Archives and Online Resources

Modern digitized collections providing access to historical materials.

Library of Congress, Chronicling America Digital Newspaper Collection. newspapers.loc.gov

Relevance: Digitized newspapers from the period providing contemporary reactions to Bly's work from various regional perspectives.

Theme Connection: Documents the reaction phase across different communities and regions.

Heinz History Center, Pittsburgh. Nellie Bly Digital Collection. heinzhistorycenter.org

Relevance: Comprehensive digital archive of Bly materials including photographs, articles, and personal items.

Theme Connection: Visual and textual evidence supporting all aspects of the Revolution, Reaction, Reform theme.

Source Evaluation and Methodology

Primary Source Analysis

The primary sources, particularly Bly's own writings, provide authentic insight into her motivations and methods. These sources are evaluated for:

  • Authenticity: Verified through multiple archival collections
  • Bias: Bly's advocacy perspective acknowledged and balanced with contemporary reactions
  • Historical Context: Interpreted within the social and political climate of the 1880s-1890s

Secondary Source Reliability

Secondary sources are selected based on:

  • Scholarly Credentials: Peer-reviewed publications and respected historians
  • Use of Primary Sources: Extensive citation of original documents
  • Balanced Perspective: Consideration of multiple viewpoints and criticisms

Research Limitations

This research acknowledges certain limitations:

  • Some contemporary reactions may be underrepresented due to limited preservation of working-class perspectives
  • International reactions to Bly's world journey could benefit from more non-English language sources
  • Long-term impact assessment relies heavily on secondary analysis due to temporal distance

Note on Citations

All sources follow Chicago Manual of Style format as recommended for National History Day projects. Page numbers and specific dates are provided where available. Digital sources include access dates to ensure reliability.
