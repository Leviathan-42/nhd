About Nellie Bly

Elizabeth Cochrane Seaman, better known by her pen name Nellie Bly, was an American journalist who pioneered investigative journalism. Born in 1864, she is best known for her record-breaking trip around the world in 72 days and her exposé on the conditions of asylum patients.

Her fearless reporting and dedication to uncovering the truth made her a revolutionary figure in journalism and women's rights.

Revolution

Breaking Barriers

Nellie Bly revolutionized journalism by becoming one of the first female investigative reporters in an era when women were largely excluded from newsrooms.

Undercover Reporting

She pioneered immersive journalism techniques, going undercover to expose injustices firsthand.

Around the World

Her 72-day journey around the world in 1889-1890 broke records and challenged perceptions of what women could achieve.

Reaction

Public Response

Nellie Bly's work sparked immediate public outcry and debate. Her exposé "Ten Days in a Mad-House" shocked readers with its revelations about the treatment of mentally ill patients.

While many praised her courage, others criticized her methods and questioned whether women should engage in such dangerous work.

"I have never written a word that did not come from my heart. I never shall." - Nellie Bly

Reform

Mental Health Reform

Her asylum exposé led to a grand jury investigation and $1 million increase in the budget for mental health institutions in New York.

Labor Rights

Bly's reporting on factory conditions helped spark discussions about worker safety and fair wages.

Women's Rights

Her success paved the way for future generations of female journalists and professionals.
